Introduction

This document contains the main model for RI-CLPM analysis for article about social dominance orientations and trust in trade unions. First, fit measures are showed for all calculated models. Second, fit mesasures of unconstrained and constrained models are compared. Third, fit measures are compared between models. Fourth, coefficients of the chosen model are interpretated. At the end of the document principal findigs are synthetized.

Analysis

Fit measures

At the Table Table 1 fit measures of all calculated models are showed. In general, all models shows good fit measures, being CFI > 0.9.

At the Table Table 2 fit measures of unconstrained and constrained models are showed. Following Rutkowski & Svetina (2014) and Chen (2007) all differences suggest that unconstrained models should be retained (ΔCFI < 0.02, ΔRMSEA < 0.03).

At the Table Table 3 comparisson between models are showed. Following Rutkowski & Svetina (2014) and Chen (2007), al comparisson meet model invarance (ΔCFI < 0.02, ΔRMSEA < 0.03). Therefore, the most parsimonious models can be retained (d2).

At the Table ?@tbl-bw1, estimates for the random intercepts of unconstrained models are showed. Both random intercepts have significant variance. This implies that there are stable differences between individuals with respect to their expected scores in X and Y1.

Also, there is a correlation between random intercepts of both variables. This implies that people who X, tends to have less/more Y.

At the Table Table 4 estimates of the crosslagged regressions are showed for the unconstrained models. The analysis reveals a positive/negative effect between X and Y at waves Z. In details, a deviation of one point above the average level of X at wave Z predicts a deviation of 0.014 in Y during wave Z+1, controlling for deviations from the expected score in Y.

At the Table Table 5 estimates for the random intercepts of constrained models are showed. Both random intercepts have significant variance. This implies that there are stable differences between individuals with respect to their expected scores in X and Y.

Correlation between random intercepts also can be found in the d2 model.

At the Table Table 6 estimates of the crosslagged regressions are showed for the constrained models. It is observed that a deviation of one point above the average level of X predicts a deviation of -0.022 in Y during the next measurement, controlling for deviations from the expected score in Y.
